Dislocations
01
Patellar dislocation
Young women, athletes, hyperlaxity -- often recurrent and under-treated
Dislocation

Almost always lateral. May reduce spontaneously before arrival -- patient presents with haemarthrosis and medial retinaculum tenderness. Trochlear dysplasia and MPFL laxity underlie recurrent cases. Distinct from knee dislocation (tibiofemoral), which is far more serious.

Evaluation
  • Patella visibly and palpably displaced laterally
  • Knee held in slight flexion, unwilling to straighten
  • Medial retinaculum tenderness on palpation
  • Apprehension sign at 30 deg flexion
  • X-ray: AP + lateral + Merchant view -- exclude fracture
  • Osteochondral fragment in effusion on Merchant view
Management
  • Reduce promptly -- most patients need no sedation (Merck)
  • Post-reduction: assess stability through ROM
  • Knee immobiliser if unstable; elastic bandage if stable
  • Ortho referral first episode -- assess MPFL, trochlea
  • MRI if haemarthrosis, osteochondral injury, or instability
ER Pain Management
Most patients require no analgesia for reduction (Merck). For anxious patients: intra-articular lidocaine 1% 10-20 mL via lateral approach, wait 5 min. IV morphine 0.05-0.1 mg/kg or IN fentanyl 1.5 mcg/kg if needed prior to block.
Reduction -- flex hip, push patella medially, extend knee
Patient supine. Slightly flex the hip first -- this relaxes quadriceps tension. Then gently push the patella medially with your thumbs while simultaneously extending the knee. A palpable clunk confirms reduction. Patella often reduces incidentally when the knee is extended. No force required.
YouTube Google Video Merck Manuals LITFL
Don't miss
MPFL tear on first dislocation -- MRI. Multiple episodes -- ortho for MPFL reconstruction. Osteochondral loose bodies need arthroscopic removal. Do not confuse with knee (tibiofemoral) dislocation -- a vascular emergency.
02
Anterior shoulder dislocation
Most common large-joint dislocation -- Cunningham technique almost never taught to GPs
Dislocation

95% anterior subcoracoid. Arm in slight abduction and external rotation. Check axillary nerve before and after -- light touch sensation over the lateral deltoid (badge area). Hemarthrosis within 48 h post-reduction suggests recurrent dislocation or capsular injury.

Evaluation
  • "Squared off" shoulder contour
  • Anterior fullness, posterior hollow
  • Axillary nerve: lateral arm sensation (badge area)
  • Axillary artery: radial pulse, cap refill
  • X-ray: AP + Y-view (confirms anterior)
  • Post-reduction X-ray mandatory
Management
  • Reduce early -- muscle spasm worsens fast
  • Sling 2-3 weeks; longer in young patients
  • Recheck axillary nerve and pulse post-reduction
  • Ortho if greater than 40 yrs, Hill-Sachs, or recurrent
  • MRI if neurovascular deficit or Bankart suspected
  • Haemarthrosis within 48 h post-reduction: re-aspirate
ER Pain Management
Cunningham: only IV diazepam 2-5 mg + massage -- no procedural sedation needed. Intra-articular lidocaine 1% 20 mL, posterior approach below posterior acromion -- equivalent to procedural sedation with lower risk. Ketamine 0.2-0.3 mg/kg IV if others fail. Adequate sedation and analgesia are key for all other techniques (Merck).
Cunningham Technique -- no sedation needed
Patient seated, arm hanging at side. Massage biceps, deltoid, trapezius 2-5 min until muscles visibly relax. Ask patient to slowly flex elbow to 90 deg. Reduction occurs spontaneously. No traction, no force. Alternatively: traction-countertraction with sheets -- lean bodyweight backward, not arm strength (Merck). Hennepin (external rotation) and Stimson (prone hanging) are also valid options.

Don't miss
Axillary nerve injury, greater tuberosity fracture (post-reduction X-ray), rotator cuff tear in patients over 40 yrs (MRI if persistent weakness). First dislocation in young patient = high recurrence risk -- discuss Bankart lesion with ortho.
03
Posterior shoulder dislocation
Missed in up to ~50% of cases -- the light bulb sign most ER doctors forget
Dislocation

Accounts for only 2-4% of shoulder dislocations. Classic triad: seizure / electrocution / high-energy trauma. AP X-ray looks almost normal. Arm locked in internal rotation is the one sign you cannot miss. An AP film alone is not adequate to exclude this diagnosis.

Incidence2-4% of all shoulder dislocations
Miss rateUp to ~50% at first presentation
MechanismSeizure · Electrocution · High-energy
Evaluation
  • Arm locked in IR -- cannot externally rotate
  • Loss of anterior shoulder fullness (subtle)
  • Light bulb sign on AP (humeral head round/symmetric)
  • Trough sign: vertical line = impression fracture of anterior head
  • Axillary view or Y-view confirms posterior -- mandatory
  • AP alone is NOT adequate to rule out posterior dislocation
  • CT post-reduction: assess reverse Hill-Sachs bone loss
Management
  • Procedural sedation required
  • Immobilise in external rotation post-reduction (not sling)
  • Ortho mandatory -- reverse Hill-Sachs common
  • High recurrence risk -- surgical planning often needed
  • Delayed reduction (over 3 weeks): surgical
ER Pain Management
Ketamine 1-1.5 mg/kg IV + midazolam 0.03 mg/kg, or propofol 1 mg/kg titrated. Intra-articular injection of local anaesthetic plus procedural sedation recommended (Merck). Sheet-based traction-countertraction technique same as anterior -- but abduct and internally rotate to disengage humeral head first.
Axial traction + lateral pressure + gentle external rotation
Sheet traction-countertraction: sheet around upper torso under the axilla, tied around assistant's hips for counter-traction. Operator applies axial traction to the arm. Fully abduct and internally rotate to disengage the head from the glenoid rim, then gently externally rotate. Never force ER without axial traction first. Reduction confirmed by restoration of round shoulder contour and return of ER range.

Don't miss
Seizure patient holding arm in internal rotation = axillary view, always. AP film alone not sufficient. Missed posteriors over 3 weeks require surgical reduction. Often accompanied by fracture of humeral neck or tuberosities -- look carefully on CT.
04
Finger PIP joint dislocation
Dismissed as "jammed finger" -- missed volar plate injuries cause permanent stiffness
Dislocation

Most PIP dislocations are dorsal (hyperextension mechanism). The volar plate may avulse from the middle phalanx base -- seen as a small bone fragment on lateral X-ray. Critically: assess lateral ligament stability after every PIP reduction by stress testing. Never just buddy tape and discharge.

Evaluation
  • AP, lateral, and oblique X-rays -- lateral views with digit separated
  • Lateral X-ray: volar plate avulsion fragment at base of middle phalanx
  • Post-reduction: stability testing of ALL PIP dislocations (Merck)
  • Test lateral ligaments by stress in radial and ulnar directions
  • Check for boutonniere -- central slip injury, extension lag at PIP
Management
  • Dorsal: reduce then buddy tape in 30 deg flexion
  • Volar plate avulsion: extension block splint 4-6 weeks
  • Volar dislocation (rare): involves central slip -- splint in full extension
  • Irreducible (volar plate interposition): surgical referral
  • Extension lag at PIP post-reduction: hand surgery same day
ER Pain Management
Digital nerve block: lidocaine 1% without adrenaline, 1-2 mL each side at base of finger. Wait 3-5 min. Complete analgesia and muscle relaxation. Merck recommends digital nerve block for most PIP dislocations before reduction.
Traction then flex -- do NOT hyperextend first
Long-axis traction on the finger, then gently flex the middle phalanx while maintaining traction. Hyperextending first significantly worsens volar plate damage. One smooth motion after digital block. Post-reduction: mandatory stress testing of lateral ligaments (Merck).
YouTube Google Video Merck Manuals
Don't miss
Volar dislocation = central slip rupture = boutonniere if missed. Extension lag at PIP post-reduction = hand surgery same day. Volar plate avulsion with more than 30% articular surface involvement may need surgical fixation.
05
Nursemaid's elbow -- radial head subluxation
Toddler, limp arm after wrist pull -- hyperpronation takes 3 seconds
Dislocation

Age 1-4 years, arm pulled by the wrist -- the annular ligament slips and becomes entrapped between radial head and capitellum. Arm hangs limp in pronation. Clinical diagnosis -- X-ray normal and generally unnecessary with a clear traction history. The manoeuvre itself is diagnostic and therapeutic (Merck).

HyperpronationHigher first-attempt success (Merck)
Peak age2-3 years old
MechanismAxial traction on forearm -- pulling, swinging
Evaluation
  • Age 1-4, traction or rotational injury to the forearm
  • Arm limp, pronated, no active supination
  • Radial head only mildly tender if at all
  • X-ray only if signs of other injury (swelling, bruising, deformity)
Management
  • Reduce immediately -- no anaesthesia, no immobilisation needed
  • Normal arm use within 10-15 min post-reduction
  • X-ray if still not using arm at 30 min
  • Counsel carers: use axilla when lifting, avoid axial traction on wrist
Pain Management
No analgesia needed -- both techniques cause mild pain only for a few seconds (Merck). Oral sucrose for distraction in very young. Reduction provides instant relief -- child stops crying within 30 seconds.
Hyperpronation -- thumb over radial head
Support the elbow, thumb over radial head. With the other hand, firmly and quickly hyperponate the forearm in one motion. Feel or hear a subtle click at the radial head = reduction. Thumb over the radial head facilitates recognition of the click (Merck). Child reaches for a toy within minutes.

Don't miss
Recurrent subluxations are common -- the annular ligament becomes lax. Counsel carers to use the axilla when picking up the child and avoid pulling by wrist. No immobilisation required after successful reduction (Merck).
06
TMJ dislocation -- mandibular dislocation
Mouth locked open after a yawn or dental procedure -- wrap your thumbs before reducing
Dislocation

Condylar head slips anterior to the articular eminence and locks there due to masseteric/pterygoid spasm. Usually occurs in people with a history of such dislocation. Triggers: wide yawn, biting into hard food, dental procedure, prolonged intubation, seizure. Can typically be reduced without procedural sedation unless delayed or uncooperative (Merck).

DirectionAnterior -- condyle past eminence
LateralityBilateral or unilateral
Key triggerPrior dislocation history -- lax TMJ ligaments
Evaluation
  • Mouth fixed open -- cannot close at all
  • Bilateral preauricular depression (empty glenoid fossa)
  • Mandible protruding anteriorly, drooling
  • Jaw deviates to unaffected side if unilateral
  • CT for traumatic dislocation -- exclude condylar fracture
  • Closed reduction may fail and require GA if chronic (Merck)
Management
  • Reduce promptly -- typically without sedation if acute
  • Barton bandage (chin sling) 2-3 days post-reduction (Merck)
  • Avoid wide mouth opening for at least 6 weeks (Merck)
  • Place fist under chin to prevent yawning wide
  • Food cut into small pieces; soft diet
  • Chronic recurrent dislocators: maxillofacial referral for eminectomy
ER Pain Management -- muscle relaxation is the goal
Oral or IV benzodiazepines (diazepam or midazolam) for anxiolysis and spasm relief. Intra-articular block (Merck): 1 mL lidocaine 2% per joint, 2.5 cm anterior to tragus just below zygomatic arch -- palpate the TMJ space, insert needle perpendicularly ~0.5 cm into joint, aspirate first, inject slowly, massage to hasten onset. Occasionally 2-5 mL lidocaine 2% into lateral pterygoid insertion allows spontaneous reduction. Opioid (fentanyl) may be added to benzodiazepines if needed (Merck).
Hippocratic bimanual -- downward then posterior
Patient seated, head stabilised against headrest. Wear gloves. Place thumbs on the external oblique ridge lateral to the third molars (NOT on the molar occlusal surfaces -- reduces bite injury risk). Fingers cup the mandible below. Apply firm downward pressure to bring the condyle below the eminence, then simultaneously push posteriorly into the fossa. Steady sustained pressure -- do not force -- wait for spasm to release. Click = reduction, mouth closes.
YouTube Google Video Merck Manuals ALiEM
Don't miss
Merck: thumbs on external oblique ridge (lateral to third molars), not on the biting surface -- reduces thumb injury risk. Exclude condylar fracture on CT before reducing if there is a trauma mechanism. Mandibular fracture may only become apparent when pain persists after TMJ reduction. Chronic dislocation over 2-3 weeks cannot be reduced in ED -- refer maxillofacial surgery.
07
Elbow dislocation
Second most common large-joint dislocation -- terrible triad lurks behind a simple-looking X-ray
Dislocation

~90% posterior or posterolateral -- olecranon driven behind the humerus after FOOSH. Reduction should be attempted soon, ideally within 30 minutes of diagnosis (Merck). Open dislocations require surgery but closed reduction should still be performed as interim if neurovascular deficit is present.

Type~90% posterior/posterolateral
Vascular riskBrachial artery 5-13%
Nerve at riskMedian (AIN) and ulnar nerve
Reduce within30 minutes of diagnosis (Merck)
Evaluation
  • Elbow at ~45 deg flexion, olecranon prominent posteriorly
  • Loss of equilateral triangle (olecranon + both epicondyles)
  • Radial pulse, capillary refill, temp -- brachial artery
  • Median nerve: thenar sensation, OK sign (AIN = thumb IP + index DIP)
  • Ulnar nerve: hypothenar sensation, dorsum 1st web space (radial nerve)
  • X-ray AP + lateral -- rule out fracture
  • Terrible triad = dislocation + radial head + coronoid fracture
Management
  • Reduce promptly ideally within 30 min (Merck)
  • Above-elbow backslab at 90 deg flexion post-reduction
  • Post-reduction X-ray -- confirm concentric reduction
  • Repeat full neurovascular exam after reduction
  • Absent radial pulse post-reduction = vascular surgery immediately
  • Ortho referral all cases
  • Terrible triad: surgical fixation required
ER Pain Management
PSA usually required (Merck). Lateral soft-spot intra-articular block: locate the centre of the triangle formed by radial head, lateral olecranon, and lateral humeral epicondyle -- insert needle perpendicularly, aspirate blood first, then inject 10-15 mL lidocaine 1%. May permit lower PSA dosing. IV morphine + midazolam if block alone insufficient.
Prone hanging (preferred solo) or supine traction-countertraction
Prone (Merck preferred): patient prone on stretcher, elbow flexed, forearm dangling over edge. Raise stretcher to your pelvic level. Apply distal traction (gravity + gentle downward pressure on olecranon). Forearm lengthening + clunk = reduction. Supine: assistant countertraction across anterior arm. Operator applies axial wrist traction then downward pressure on proximal forearm while gently flexing elbow. If not reducing: assistant lifts humerus while you maintain downward olecranon pressure.
YouTube Google Video Merck Manuals LITFL
Don't miss
Terrible triad -- looks simple on plain X-ray, requires surgical fixation, disastrous outcome if missed. Absent radial pulse = vascular emergency. AIN injury: patient cannot form "OK" sign. Open dislocations = surgery, but still reduce as interim if neurovascular deficit (Merck).
08
Hip dislocation -- posterior and anterior
AVN clock starts at injury -- imaging first, reduce within 1 hour, no exceptions
Dislocation

High-energy trauma -- classic dashboard injury (axial load on flexed hip). ~90% posterior. AVN risk rises steeply after 6 hours. Sciatic nerve (peroneal division) at risk posteriorly; femoral artery and nerve anteriorly.

Type~90% posterior, ~10% anterior
AVN riskRises sharply after 6 hours
Nerve (post.)Sciatic -- peroneal division
Vessel (ant.)Femoral artery and nerve
Posterior (90%)
  • Hip: flexed, adducted, internally rotated
  • Leg appears shortened
  • Sciatic: EHL strength, dorsal foot sensation
  • Posterior wall acetabular fracture common
Anterior (10%)
  • Hip: extended, abducted, externally rotated
  • Leg may appear lengthened
  • Femoral nerve and femoral artery at risk
  • Obturator type: groin mass, hip in flexion + ER
Evaluation -- both types
  • Full trauma assessment -- always high-energy mechanism
  • Sciatic (post.): active dorsiflexion, EHL, dorsal foot sensation
  • Femoral (ant.): quads strength, femoral pulse, capillary refill
  • X-ray AP pelvis + lateral -- confirm direction, exclude femoral neck fracture
  • CT post-reduction mandatory -- intra-articular fragments and acetabular pattern
ER Pain Management
IV morphine 0.1 mg/kg immediately. Procedural sedation mandatory: ketamine 1-1.5 mg/kg IV + midazolam 0.03 mg/kg, or propofol 1-2 mg/kg titrated. Failed closed reduction = GA in theatre. FICB adjunct: 30-40 mL ropivacaine 0.375% or bupivacaine 0.25% -- significantly reduces post-reduction morphine consumption.
Posterior -- Allis technique (Merck) or Captain Morgan (solo)
Allis (Merck): assistant applies downward pressure on both iliac spines (or sheet secured to stretcher). Operator at foot of bed: flex hip and knee to 90 deg, apply axial traction upward. Press knee medially to adduct and foot laterally to internally rotate. Then raise shoulder while pulling down on ankle to apply traction. Gradually rotate femur back and forth (Merck). Captain Morgan (solo): operator's knee under patient's popliteal fossa as fulcrum, lift upward.
YouTube -- Allis YouTube -- Capt. Morgan Google Video Merck Manuals LITFL
Anterior -- traction in deformity line, then IR, then adduct
Assistant stabilises pelvis. Traction in line of deformity (abducted + ER), then progressively internally rotate while maintaining traction, then adduct. More force required -- ensure deep sedation. Confirm femoral pulse immediately after reduction.
YouTube -- anterior Google Video
Critical rules -- no exceptions
Never reduce without imaging. Femoral neck fracture mimics dislocation clinically -- reducing it as a dislocation causes catastrophic vascular disruption. Aim to reduce within 1 hour. Post-reduction CT mandatory. Sciatic foot drop: usually neurapraxia, 60-70% recover over 3-6 months.
09
Sternoclavicular joint dislocation
Posterior type is a mediastinal emergency -- trachea, great vessels, oesophagus at risk
Dislocation

Anterior dislocations are common and usually benign. Posterior dislocations are rare but life-threatening -- the clavicle head driven into the mediastinum can compress the trachea, oesophagus, subclavian artery, or brachial plexus. The importance is in the direction of dislocation: it dictates completely different management. Plain X-ray misses up to 50% of SCJ dislocations (LITFL) -- CT chest with arterial contrast is mandatory if posterior is suspected.

Anterior typeMore common -- weak anterior SCJ ligament
Posterior typeRare -- mediastinal emergency
X-ray miss rateUp to 50% on plain film (LITFL)
Anterior dislocation
  • Visible and palpable medial clavicle prominence anteriorly
  • Localised pain, arm held adducted
  • Usually managed conservatively -- figure-of-8 sling
  • Closed reduction in acute cases; high re-dislocation rate
  • Accept chronic position if recurrent
Posterior dislocation -- Emergency
  • May appear LESS prominent (mass driven inward)
  • Stridor, dysphagia, hoarseness, venous congestion
  • Upper limb ischaemia or neurological deficit
  • Dyspnoea, respiratory distress
  • Pulse oximetry both arms -- asymmetry = subclavian compromise
  • MUST be reduced in theatre with vascular surgery standby
Evaluation -- both types
  • CT chest with arterial contrast -- imaging of choice (LITFL)
  • Serendipity view (40 deg cephalad AP) if CT unavailable
  • Plain X-ray inadequate -- misses up to 50% of SCJ dislocations
  • Airway, breathing, vascular status assessment first
  • Vascular surgery on standby before any posterior reduction attempt
ER Pain Management
Anterior: oral NSAIDs + paracetamol, figure-of-8 sling, ice. Closed reduction of anterior: IV morphine 0.1 mg/kg + midazolam 0.05 mg/kg + sandbag between scapulae. Posterior: IV morphine for analgesia only -- do NOT attempt reduction outside theatre. Secure airway if stridor present. Call thoracic/vascular surgery immediately.
Anterior -- lateral traction + direct posterior pressure on medial clavicle
Patient supine, sandbag/roll between scapulae. Apply lateral traction on abducted arm while assistant applies direct posterior pressure on medial clavicle head. A click confirms reduction. High recurrence rate -- immobilise with figure-of-8 or clavicle strap 4-6 weeks.
YouTube Google Video LITFL
Critical -- never reduce posterior SCJ outside theatre
Posterior SCJ must be reduced in theatre with vascular surgery present -- reduction can lacerate the subclavian artery (LITFL). CT with arterial contrast mandatory if posterior suspected -- do not rely on plain X-ray. Stridor or venous congestion = immediate airway management and emergency surgical referral. Cardiothoracic team should be present during reduction (LITFL).
Tendon ruptures
10
Achilles tendon rupture
Patient can still plantar-flex using toe flexors -- the classic missed diagnosis
Tendon

30-50 yr old male "weekend warrior." Sudden sharp pain described as being kicked from behind. Critical trap: active plantarflexion via FHL and FDL is preserved -- rupture missed and patient sent home weight-bearing. If all 3 tests (Thompson, Matles, gap palpation) are positive, sensitivity for Achilles tear is 100% (Merck).

Evaluation
  • Palpable gap 2-6 cm above calcaneal insertion
  • Thompson (Simmonds) test: calf squeeze -- no plantarflexion = rupture
  • Matles test: prone with knee flexed 90 deg -- foot falls into neutral or dorsiflexion = positive
  • Active plantarflexion MAY still be present via FHL/FDL
  • US first-line; MRI if partial tear suspected
  • All 3 tests positive = 100% sensitivity (Merck)
Management
  • Short leg splint in plantarflexion immediately in ED (Merck)
  • Non-weight bearing, crutches
  • Urgent ortho -- surgical vs conservative decision
  • Young/active: surgical repair preferred
  • Elderly/sedentary: functional bracing acceptable
  • VACOped boot or equinus cast at 20-30 deg plantarflexion
ER Pain Management
Paracetamol 1g + ibuprofen 400 mg. Elevation, ice, crutches. Avoid long-term NSAIDs -- may impair tendon healing. Pain often settles deceptively quickly, which falsely reassures clinicians and delays referral. Fluoroquinolone and corticosteroid-associated ruptures -- rising in frequency; trivial mechanism + recent FQ course or steroid injection = Thompson test regardless.
Thompson + Matles + Gap Palpation -- 100% sensitivity when all positive
Thompson: patient prone, feet off edge, squeeze calf firmly -- no plantarflexion = positive. Matles: patient prone, flex both knees to 90 deg -- normal foot drops 20-30 deg into plantarflexion; ruptured side stays neutral or dorsiflexes. Gap palpation: patient stands on affected leg if possible; palpate tendon course for a gap.
YouTube Google Video Merck Video Merck Manuals LITFL
Don't miss
FQ (Cipro) and corticosteroid-associated ruptures are rising -- trivial mechanism + recent FQ or local steroid injection = Thompson test regardless of story. Partial tears cannot be distinguished from complete tears on Thompson test alone (Wikipedia/evidence) -- use US to confirm and characterise.
11
Quadriceps vs patellar tendon rupture
Can't extend the knee -- patella position on lateral X-ray tells you which one
Tendon

Both present with inability to extend the knee against gravity. Lateral X-ray is diagnostic: patella LOW (baja) = quadriceps ruptured. Patella HIGH (alta) = patellar tendon ruptured. Surgical window is 2 weeks -- don't delay.

Quadriceps rupture -- over 40 yrs
  • Palpable gap above patella
  • Patella LOW (baja) on lateral X-ray
  • Older adults, chronic steroid use, CKD, dialysis
  • May still SLR supine but not against gravity standing
Patellar tendon rupture -- under 40 yrs
  • Palpable gap below patella
  • Patella HIGH (alta) on lateral X-ray
  • Younger athletes, prior patellar tendinopathy
  • Often complete inability to extend
ER Pain Management
IV morphine 0.05-0.1 mg/kg. Knee immobiliser in full extension. Elevate, ice. Do not attempt reduction -- complete ruptures are a surgical emergency requiring urgent ortho referral.
Straight Leg Raise -- key bedside test
Supine -- ask patient to lift leg with knee straight. Complete failure = complete extensor mechanism rupture. Partial SLR with extension lag = partial tear. Confirm with lateral X-ray then US or MRI.
YouTube Google Video Radiopaedia
Don't miss
Surgical repair within 2 weeks for best outcomes -- missed repairs result in quadriceps atrophy and permanent extension lag. Urgent ortho referral same day. CKD and dialysis patients: highest risk for quad tendon rupture -- always examine the knee in these patients after a fall.
12
Distal biceps tendon rupture
The Popeye arm -- the hook test almost no one does in the GP room
Tendon

Males 40-60, heavy eccentric load. Audible "pop" at the antecubital fossa, biceps belly retracts proximally. Loses 40% supination strength and 30% flexion strength if not repaired within 2 weeks.

Supination strength lost~40% if unrepaired
Flexion strength lost~30% if unrepaired
Hook test specificity~100%
Surgical windowLess than 2 weeks
Evaluation
  • Popeye sign: biceps belly retracted proximally
  • Hook test: no cord to hook = complete rupture
  • Supination weakness greater than flexion weakness
  • Delayed antecubital ecchymosis at 24-48 h
  • US confirms; MRI if partial tear suspected
Management
  • Complete rupture: urgent ortho within 2 weeks
  • Partial tear: conservative physio trial first
  • Sling in 90 deg flexion for comfort
  • Elderly/low-demand: accept 30-40% supination loss
  • Monitor antecubital haematoma for neurovascular compression
ER Pain Management
Oral NSAIDs + paracetamol. Sling in 90 deg flexion, ice. Monitor antecubital haematoma for enlargement. No reduction manoeuvre -- this is a tendon rupture, not a dislocation.
Hook Test -- sensitivity 81%, specificity ~100%
Elbow at 90 degrees, forearm supinated. Hook index finger behind the lateral edge of the biceps tendon from the lateral side of the antecubital fossa. Normally hook approximately 1 cm of cord. Nothing to hook = complete rupture.
YouTube Google Video Physiopedia
Don't miss
Partial tears: persistent pain with near-full strength -- hook test may still feel a thinned cord. US is diagnostic. Delayed antecubital ecchymosis at 24-48 h is a classic sign clinicians routinely miss.
Muscle and surgical emergencies
13
Iliopsoas / hip flexor tear
Misdiagnosed as groin strain -- femoral nerve compression turns it surgical
Muscle

High-speed kick or sprint. Deep anterior groin and lower abdominal pain. Dangerous variant: large haematoma compressing the femoral nerve -- quadriceps weakness and anterior thigh numbness = surgical emergency. In anticoagulated patients, the haematoma can extend into the retroperitoneal space and be life-threatening.

Evaluation
  • Deep groin and lower abdominal pain with resisted hip flexion
  • Palpable mass in groin if haematoma present
  • Femoral nerve signs: quads weakness, anterior thigh numbness
  • MRI gold standard; US limited by depth
  • CT if anticoagulated, haematocrit dropping, or femoral nerve signs
Management
  • Conservative: rest, ice, NSAIDs 5-7 days
  • Crutches if weight-bearing painful
  • Femoral nerve signs: urgent CT/MRI and ortho/vascular immediately
  • Lesser trochanter avulsion in young/active patients: surgical
  • Avoid active hip flexion for 2-3 weeks
ER Pain Management
IV/oral NSAIDs and paracetamol. Hip flexion positioning (pillow under knees) dramatically reduces pain by offloading the iliopsoas -- simplest and most effective first intervention. Ice over lower abdomen and groin.
Resisted Hip Flexion Test and Thomas Test
Supine, knee bent -- ask patient to lift thigh against your resistance. Deep groin pain = positive. Thomas test (passive hip extension with knee extended) stretches the iliopsoas and reproduces pain in complete tears.
YouTube Google Video Physiopedia
Don't miss
Retroperitoneal haematoma in anticoagulated patients (warfarin/DOAC) mimics this exactly. Dropping haematocrit and groin pain = urgent CT abdomen/pelvis. Femoral nerve palsy = immediate surgical referral.
14
Acute compartment syndrome
6-hour window -- never elevate -- passive stretch is the earliest sign
Surgical Emergency

Most common after tibial shaft fractures, forearm fractures, and crush injuries. Fasciotomy window is 6 hours -- after that, permanent muscle necrosis. This emergency kills limbs, not patients. Rural GPs must recognise and transfer immediately.

Fasciotomy window6 hours from onset
Pressure thresholdGreater than 30 mmHg or delta-p less than 30 mmHg
The 6 Ps -- in order of onset
  • Pain out of proportion -- earliest and most important
  • Pressure -- tense, woody compartment on palpation
  • Paresthesia -- tingling in nerve distribution
  • Paralysis -- late sign, very poor prognosis
  • Pallor -- late sign
  • Pulselessness -- very late, never wait for this
GP / ER Management
  • Remove ALL circumferential dressings and casts immediately
  • DO NOT elevate the limb -- reduces perfusion pressure
  • Keep limb at heart level
  • Measure compartment pressure if equipment available
  • Call ortho NOW -- IV access, prepare for theatre
  • Transfer immediately if surgery unavailable at your facility
ER Pain Management
Do NOT withhold analgesia to preserve the sign. Diagnosis is made clinically and by compartment pressure measurement -- not by whether the patient is still screaming. IV morphine titrated. Fasciotomy is the only definitive treatment.
Passive Stretch Test -- precedes paresthesia and paralysis
Anterior leg compartment: passively plantarflex the foot. Forearm: passively extend all fingers. Severe pain with passive stretch = compartment syndrome until proven otherwise. This sign appears before the late Ps and is your earliest clinical window to act.
YouTube Google Video LITFL LITFL CCC
Absolute rules -- no exceptions
NEVER elevate. NEVER compress. NEVER cast circumferentially. A tight cast after a tibial fracture that the patient says feels too tight = compartment syndrome until proven otherwise. Fasciotomy delayed more than 6 hours = permanent disability. Transfer immediately if you cannot operate.
