🦴 Clinical Quick Reference

Orthopaedic Emergencies
GPs Need to Know

14 under-discussed presentations — evaluation, ER pain management, reduction manoeuvres, and video references. Compiled for general practitioners.

🔗 Each manoeuvre includes a YouTube search link (always live, always current) and a stable reference link from Merck Manuals, LITFL, or Medmastery. Direct YouTube video IDs expire — these never do.
Dislocations
01
Patellar dislocation
Young women, athletes, hyperlaxity — often recurrent and under-treated
Dislocation

Almost always lateral. May reduce spontaneously before arrival — patient presents with haemarthrosis and medial retinaculum tenderness saying "my kneecap popped out." Trochlear dysplasia is the underlying culprit in recurrent cases.

Evaluation
  • Visible lateral patellar displacement
  • Medial retinaculum tenderness
  • Positive apprehension sign
  • X-ray: AP + lateral + Merchant view
  • Look for osteochondral fragment in effusion
Management
  • Ice, immobilise in extension post-reduction
  • Aspirate if tense haemarthrosis
  • Knee immobiliser 2–4 weeks
  • Ortho referral on first episode
  • MRI if haemarthrosis or osteochondral injury
💊 ER Pain Management
IV morphine 0.05–0.1 mg/kg or intranasal fentanyl. Intra-articular lidocaine 1% 10–20 mL (lateral approach, wait 5 min) is highly effective and avoids the need for procedural sedation.
🖐 Reduction Manoeuvre
Fully extend the knee (relaxes quadriceps), then push the patella medially with your thumbs while the patient actively extends. One smooth, gentle motion — no force needed if the muscle is relaxed.
Search YouTube 📹 Merck Manuals video
⚠ Don't miss
MPFL tear on first dislocation → MRI. Multiple episodes → ortho for MPFL reconstruction. Osteochondral loose bodies need arthroscopic removal.
02
Anterior shoulder dislocation
Most common large-joint dislocation — but the Cunningham technique is almost never taught to GPs
Dislocation

95% anterior subcoracoid. Arm held in slight abduction and external rotation. Always check the axillary nerve before and after reduction — sensation over the deltoid badge area.

Evaluation
  • "Squared off" shoulder contour
  • Anterior fullness, posterior hollow
  • Test axillary nerve: deltoid badge sensation
  • X-ray: AP + Y-view (confirms anterior)
  • Post-reduction X-ray mandatory
Management
  • Reduce early — narrowing muscle spasm window
  • Sling 2–3 weeks (young: longer)
  • Recheck axillary nerve post-reduction
  • Ortho if >40 yrs, Hill-Sachs, or recurrent
  • MRI if neurovascular deficit or Bankart suspected
💊 ER Pain Management
Cunningham technique needs only IV diazepam 2–5 mg + massage — no procedural sedation. Alternative: intra-articular lidocaine 20 mL 1% (posterior approach, just below posterior acromion) — as effective as procedural sedation with lower risk. IV ketamine 0.2–0.3 mg/kg only if other methods unavailable.
🖐 Cunningham Technique (no sedation needed)
Patient seated, arm hanging at side. Massage biceps, deltoid, and trapezius for 2–5 min until muscles visibly relax. Ask patient to slowly flex the elbow to 90°. Reduction occurs spontaneously. No traction, no force, no drama.
Search YouTube 📹 LITFL — Cunningham 📹 Medmastery
⚠ Don't miss
Axillary nerve injury, greater tuberosity fracture (post-reduction X-ray), rotator cuff tear in >40 yrs (MRI if persistent weakness). First dislocation in young patient → high recurrence risk — discuss Bankart lesion with ortho.
03
Posterior shoulder dislocation
Missed in up to 79% of cases — the "light bulb sign" most ER doctors forget
Dislocation

Classic triad: seizure / electrocution / high-energy trauma. The AP X-ray looks almost normal — the diagnostic mistake that gets made repeatedly. Arm locked in internal rotation is the key physical finding.

Miss rate~79% missed at first presentation
MechanismSeizure · Electrocution · High-energy
Evaluation
  • Arm locked in internal rotation — cannot externally rotate
  • "Light bulb sign" on AP X-ray
  • Axillary view or Y-view confirms posterior
  • Always get axillary view after seizure or ECT
  • CT post-reduction to assess bone loss
Management
  • Procedural sedation required (more force needed)
  • Immobilise in external rotation post-reduction
  • Ortho referral mandatory — reverse Hill-Sachs common
  • High recurrence — surgical planning often needed
💊 ER Pain Management
Procedural sedation required: ketamine 1–1.5 mg/kg IV + midazolam 0.03 mg/kg, or propofol 1 mg/kg titrated. Intra-articular block is less practical here given posterior approach difficulty.
🖐 Reduction: Axial traction → lateral pressure → external rotation
Apply traction in long axis of arm. Apply lateral pressure on humeral head from posteriorly while an assistant provides counter-traction across the chest. Only then gently externally rotate. Never force external rotation without axial traction first.
Search YouTube 📹 LITFL reference
⚠ Don't miss
Seizure patient holding arm in internal rotation = get axillary view, always, no exceptions. Missed posteriors >3 weeks old require surgical reduction — the prognosis worsens dramatically.
04
Finger PIP joint dislocation
Dismissed as "jammed finger" — missed volar plate injuries cause permanent stiffness
Dislocation

Dorsal dislocation is most common (ball sports). The volar plate is the critical structure — avulsion leads to joint instability and permanent stiffness if not splinted in the correct position. Never just buddy tape and discharge.

Evaluation
  • PIP joint fixed in hyperextension
  • Lateral X-ray: volar plate avulsion fragment
  • Post-reduction: test full active ROM + lateral stability
  • Check for boutonnière (central slip injury)
Management
  • Dorsal: reduce → buddy tape in 30° flexion
  • Volar plate avulsion: extension block splint 4–6 weeks
  • Irreducible (volar plate interposition): surgical referral
  • Extension lag post-reduction: hand surgery referral
💊 ER Pain Management
Digital nerve block: lidocaine 1% (no adrenaline), 1–2 mL each side at the base of the finger. Wait 3–5 min. Provides complete analgesia and muscle relaxation — makes reduction effortless.
🖐 Traction → flex (do NOT hyperextend first)
Apply traction in the long axis of the finger, then gently flex the middle phalanx while maintaining traction. Hyperextending first significantly worsens volar plate damage. One smooth motion is all that's needed after a good digital block.
Search YouTube 📹 Merck Manuals video
⚠ Don't miss
Volar dislocation is rarer but dangerous — involves central slip rupture → boutonnière deformity if missed. Extension lag at PIP after reduction = hand surgery referral same day.
05
Nursemaid's elbow
Toddler, limp arm, pulled by the wrist — the hyperpronation trick takes 3 seconds
Dislocation

Age 1–4 years, pulled by the hand or wrist. Arm hangs limp in pronation at the side. Clinical diagnosis — the X-ray is normal. Hyperpronation technique has 94% first-attempt success vs 69% for the traditional supination-flexion method.

Hyperpronation success~94% first attempt
Supination-flexion success~69% first attempt
Evaluation
  • Age 1–4, pulled by the wrist/hand
  • Arm limp at side, pronated, no active supination
  • No swelling, bruising, or bony tenderness
  • X-ray only if concern for fracture
Management
  • Reduce immediately — no anaesthesia needed
  • Normal arm use within 10–15 min post-reduction
  • X-ray if still not using at 30 min
  • Counsel parents: no pulling by wrist for 6 months
💊 Pain Management
No analgesia needed. Oral sucrose for distraction in very young children. The reduction itself provides instant relief — child typically stops crying within 30 seconds of a successful reduction and reaches for a toy.
🖐 Hyperpronation (preferred — 3 seconds)
Support the elbow with one hand, thumb over the radial head. With the other hand firmly and quickly hyperponate the forearm in a single motion. Feel or hear a subtle click over the radial head = reduction.
Search YouTube 📹 Merck Manuals video
⚠ Don't miss
3+ recurrent subluxations = annular ligament laxity. Counsel parents to avoid lifting by wrists until age 5–6. Each recurrence makes the next one more likely.
06
TMJ dislocation
Mouth locked open — the patient who can't close their jaw, often after a big yawn or dental procedure
Dislocation

The condylar head slips anterior to the articular eminence and becomes locked there by masseteric and pterygoid muscle spasm. Almost always bilateral. Typically after a wide yawn, dental procedure, prolonged intubation, excessive laughter, or seizure. The longer it sits, the harder it is to reduce — get on it within the hour. Recurrent dislocators often learn to self-reduce.

DirectionAnterior (condyle locks in front of eminence)
LateralityMost commonly bilateral
Key risk factorJoint laxity · Prior dislocation · Sedation
Evaluation
  • Mouth fixed open — patient cannot close jaw
  • Marked preauricular depression bilaterally (empty glenoid fossa)
  • Anterior protrusion of mandible
  • Drooling, difficulty speaking, distress
  • Bilateral: jaw deviates midline if unilateral
  • X-ray: Towne's view or OPG confirms condyle position
  • CT if trauma, fracture, or failed reduction suspected
Management
  • Reduce as soon as possible — spasm worsens rapidly
  • Soft diet + jaw restriction 2 weeks post-reduction
  • Barton bandage (chin sling) for 24–48 h to prevent re-dislocation
  • Advise against wide opening for 4–6 weeks
  • Recurrent dislocators: refer maxillofacial / oral surgery
  • Chronic (>weeks): surgical reduction required
💊 ER Pain Management
Muscle relaxation is the goal — not just analgesia. Option 1 (preferred): IV midazolam 1–2 mg slow IV — provides anxiolysis and muscle relaxation, often enough for easy reduction with no sedation. Option 2: IV diazepam 5–10 mg. Option 3 — intra-articular block: inject 1–2 mL lidocaine 2% into each TMJ (landmark: 1 cm anterior to tragus, just below the zygomatic arch) — blocks the joint and relaxes spasm. Avoid opioids alone — they don't relax masticatory muscle spasm. Methocarbamol (muscle relaxant) is an option in some settings.
🖐 Standard technique — Hippocratic / bimanual method
Patient seated upright, head supported against the wall or headrest. Stand in front or slightly to the side. Wrap your thumbs in gauze (the jaw will clamp shut on reduction — protect your thumbs). Place both thumbs on the lower molar teeth bilaterally, fingers cupping the mandible from below. Apply firm, steady downward pressure on the molars (pushing the condyle below the eminence), then simultaneously push posteriorly to guide the condyle back into the fossa. A click or clunk confirms reduction — the mouth closes. Do not use force; use steady, sustained pressure and wait for the spasm to release.
Search YouTube 📹 Merck Manuals video 📹 LITFL reference
🖐 Wrist pivot technique (safer for operator)
An elegant alternative that protects the clinician's thumbs. Place your fists on the patient's lower molars (knuckles down) instead of thumbs. Apply the same downward + posterior force using your wrists as a fulcrum. The patient's jaw closes around your knuckles rather than trapping your thumbs — particularly useful for agitated or uncooperative patients.
Search YouTube — wrist pivot
⚠ Don't miss
Protect your thumbs — on successful reduction the masseter contracts powerfully and the jaw snaps shut with significant force. Gauze wrapping is not optional. Condylar fracture must be excluded before reduction if there is a trauma mechanism — a fracture can look identical clinically. Recurrent dislocators (>3 episodes) need maxillofacial referral for eminoplasty or capsular tightening. Chronic dislocation (>2–3 weeks) is fibrosed and cannot be reduced in the ED — refer to maxillofacial surgery.
06
Elbow dislocation
Second most common large-joint dislocation — check brachial artery and median nerve before and after
Dislocation

Second most common large-joint dislocation after the shoulder. 90% are posterior or posterolateral — the olecranon driven behind the humerus after a FOOSH with the elbow partially flexed. Muscle spasm develops rapidly; reduce early. The brachial artery and median nerve are at risk.

Type~90% posterior / posterolateral
Vascular riskBrachial artery ~5–13%
Nerve at riskMedian nerve (AIN) · Ulnar nerve
Evaluation
  • Elbow held at ~45° flexion, prominent olecranon posteriorly
  • Loss of equilateral triangle (olecranon + both epicondyles)
  • Radial pulse + capillary refill — brachial artery at risk
  • Median nerve: index/middle sensation, OK sign (AIN)
  • Ulnar nerve: little finger sensation, interosseous strength
  • X-ray AP + lateral — rule out fracture-dislocation
  • Terrible triad = dislocation + radial head + coronoid fracture
Management
  • Reduce promptly — muscle spasm worsens within minutes
  • Above-elbow backslab at 90° flexion post-reduction
  • Post-reduction X-ray mandatory (confirm concentric reduction)
  • Repeat full neurovascular exam after reduction
  • Absent radial pulse post-reduction: vascular surgery stat
  • Ortho referral all cases — assess instability and fractures
  • Terrible triad: surgical fixation required
💊 ER Pain Management
IV morphine 0.05–0.1 mg/kg + midazolam 0.03 mg/kg for muscle relaxation. Lateral soft-spot haematoma block is an excellent alternative: inject 10–15 mL lidocaine 1% into the triangular soft spot between the lateral epicondyle, radial head, and olecranon tip — wait 5–10 min. Provides good analgesia and muscle relaxation without systemic sedation.
🖐 Reduction — traction-countertraction (two-person)
Supine method: assistant applies countertraction across the anterior arm. Operator grasps the wrist and applies steady axial traction distally, then uses the other hand to apply downward pressure on the proximal forearm while gently flexing the elbow. A clunk confirms reduction. Prone hanging method (single operator): patient prone, arm dangling off the bed with a 2–4 kg weight at the wrist — gravity and muscle fatigue reduce the joint within minutes. Elegant and low-force.
Search YouTube 📹 Merck Manuals video 📹 LITFL reference
⚠ Don't miss
Terrible triad (dislocation + radial head fracture + coronoid fracture) — looks like a simple dislocation on plain X-ray, requires surgical fixation, disastrous outcome if missed. Absent radial pulse post-reduction = vascular surgery emergency. Median nerve AIN injury: patient cannot form an "OK" sign — flexion of thumb IP and index DIP joints is lost.
07
Hip dislocation — posterior & anterior
AVN of the femoral head begins at 6 hours — reduce within the hour, imaging first
Dislocation

High-energy trauma — the classic is a dashboard injury (knee strikes dash → posterior dislocation). 90% are posterior. Anterior dislocations occur with forced abduction and external rotation. Both are vascular emergencies: the lateral epiphyseal vessels are compromised at the time of dislocation, and avascular necrosis (AVN) risk climbs steeply after 6 hours. The sciatic nerve (especially the peroneal division) is at immediate risk in posterior dislocations.

Type split~90% posterior · ~10% anterior
AVN risk windowRises sharply after 6 hours
Nerve at risk (post.)Sciatic — peroneal division
Vessel at risk (ant.)Femoral artery / vein
Posterior (90%) — classic posture
  • Hip: flexed · adducted · internally rotated
  • Leg appears shortened
  • Mechanism: axial load on flexed hip (dashboard)
  • Sciatic nerve: check foot dorsiflexion (EHL), dorsal foot sensation
  • Associated posterior wall acetabular fracture common
Anterior (10%) — classic posture
  • Hip: extended · abducted · externally rotated
  • Leg may appear lengthened
  • Mechanism: forced abduction/ER — motorcycle, sports, obstetric
  • Femoral nerve + femoral artery at risk
  • Obturator type: groin mass, hip in flexion + ER
Evaluation — both types
  • Full trauma assessment first — this is always a high-energy injury
  • Check sciatic nerve (post.): active dorsiflexion, sensation dorsum foot, hallux extension
  • Check femoral nerve/vessels (ant.): quads strength, femoral pulse, cap refill
  • X-ray AP pelvis + lateral hip — confirm direction, look for acetabular/femoral neck fracture
  • CT post-reduction: mandatory — intra-articular fragments, acetabular fracture pattern
💊 ER Pain Management
IV morphine 0.1 mg/kg titrated immediately. Procedural sedation is mandatory for reduction — muscle relaxation is non-negotiable: ketamine 1–1.5 mg/kg IV + midazolam 0.03 mg/kg, or propofol 1–2 mg/kg titrated. Failed closed reduction under sedation = general anaesthesia in theatre. Fascia iliaca compartment block (FICB) as an excellent adjunct: 30–40 mL ropivacaine 0.375% or bupivacaine 0.25% — significantly reduces morphine consumption and provides post-reduction analgesia.
🖐 Posterior — Allis technique (preferred)
Patient supine, trolley lowered to the floor. Assistant applies firm downward pressure on both iliac crests to stabilise the pelvis. Operator stands on the trolley above the patient, flexes both the hip and knee to 90°. Apply steady upward traction in line with the femur (lift straight up toward the ceiling), then gently internally rotate and adduct. A satisfying clunk confirms reduction. Captain Morgan (single operator): operator places their knee under the patient's popliteal fossa as a fulcrum and lifts — useful when no assistant is available.
YouTube — Allis technique YouTube — Captain Morgan
🖐 Anterior — longitudinal traction + internal rotation
Patient supine, assistant stabilises the pelvis. Apply traction in the line of the deformity (leg is abducted + externally rotated), then progressively internally rotate the femur while maintaining traction, then gently adduct. This guides the femoral head back over the anterior acetabular rim into the socket. Anterior dislocations typically require more force — ensure deep sedation. Confirm femoral pulse immediately after reduction.
YouTube — anterior hip 📹 LITFL — Hip dislocation 📹 Merck Manuals video
⚠ Critical rules — no exceptions
Never reduce without imaging first. A femoral neck fracture can mimic a dislocation clinically — reducing a neck fracture as a dislocation causes catastrophic vascular disruption. 6-hour AVN rule: aim to reduce within 1 hour of arrival — every hour of delay matters. Post-reduction CT is mandatory — intra-articular bony fragments prevent concentric reduction and destroy articular cartilage if missed. Sciatic nerve foot drop post-reduction: usually neurapraxia, 60–70% recover — monitor carefully over 3–6 months.
Tendon ruptures
06
Achilles tendon rupture
Patient can still plantar-flex using toe flexors — the classic missed diagnosis
Tendon

30–50 year old male "weekend warrior." Sudden sharp pain described as being kicked from behind. The critical trap: the patient can still plantarflex using FHL and FDL, so the rupture is missed and they're sent home weight-bearing.

Evaluation
  • Palpable gap 2–6 cm above calcaneal insertion
  • Thompson test: calf squeeze → no plantarflexion = rupture
  • Active plantarflexion may STILL BE PRESENT (FHL/FDL)
  • US first-line (dynamic); MRI if partial tear
Management
  • Equinus cast or VACOped boot 20–30° plantarflexion
  • Non-weight bearing 2 weeks, then progressive WB
  • Urgent ortho referral — timing matters
  • Young/active → surgical repair preferred
  • Elderly/sedentary → functional bracing acceptable
💊 ER Pain Management
Paracetamol 1g + ibuprofen 400 mg (if no contraindication). Elevation, ice, crutches. Avoid long-term NSAIDs — they may impair tendon healing. Pain often settles quickly after the acute event, which falsely reassures clinicians.
🖐 Thompson Test — nearly 100% sensitive
Patient prone, feet hanging off the edge of the bed. Squeeze the calf muscle belly firmly. Normal: foot plantarflexes. Ruptured Achilles: no movement at all. Simple, reproducible, and almost infallible.
Search YouTube 📹 Merck Manuals
⚠ Don't miss
Fluoroquinolone (Cipro) and corticosteroid-associated ruptures — increasing in frequency. Trivial mechanism + recent FQ course or local steroid injection → Thompson test regardless of story.
07
Quadriceps vs patellar tendon rupture
Patient can't extend the knee — patella position on X-ray tells you which one
Tendon

Both present with inability to extend the knee against gravity. The lateral X-ray gives the answer: patella LOW = quadriceps tendon ruptured. Patella HIGH = patellar tendon ruptured. Surgical window is 2 weeks — don't delay.

Quadriceps rupture — >40 yrs
  • Palpable gap above patella
  • Patella LOW (patella baja) on lateral X-ray
  • Older adults, steroid use, CKD, renal dialysis
  • May still SLR supine but not against gravity
Patellar tendon rupture — <40 yrs
  • Palpable gap below patella
  • Patella HIGH (patella alta) on lateral X-ray
  • Younger athletes, prior patellar tendinopathy
  • Often complete inability to extend
💊 ER Pain Management
IV morphine 0.05–0.1 mg/kg. Knee immobiliser in full extension. Elevate, ice. Do NOT attempt reduction — complete ruptures are a surgical emergency. Immobilise and refer urgently.
🖐 Straight Leg Raise — the key bedside test
Supine — ask patient to lift the whole leg keeping the knee straight. Complete inability = complete extensor mechanism rupture. Partial SLR with lag = partial tear. Confirm with lateral X-ray and US or MRI.
Search YouTube 📹 Radiopaedia reference
⚠ Don't miss
Surgical repair must occur within 2 weeks for best functional outcomes. Missed repairs result in quadriceps atrophy and permanent extension lag. Treat as urgent ortho referral — same day if possible.
08
Distal biceps tendon rupture
The "Popeye arm" — the hook test almost no one does in the GP room
Tendon

Males 40–60, heavy eccentric load. Audible "pop" at the antecubital fossa, biceps muscle belly retracts proximally into a visible "Popeye" lump. Loses 40% of supination strength and 30% of flexion strength if not repaired within 2 weeks.

Supination strength lost~40% if unrepairedl
Hook test specificity~100%
Surgical window< 2 weeks
Evaluation
  • Popeye sign: biceps belly retracts proximally
  • Hook test: no cord to hook = complete rupture
  • Weakness of supination > weakness of flexion
  • Delayed ecchymosis at antecubital fossa (24–48 h)
  • US confirms; MRI if partial tear suspected
Management
  • Complete rupture: urgent ortho within 2 weeks
  • Partial tear: conservative physio trial first
  • Sling in 90° flexion for comfort
  • Elderly/low-demand: accept 30–40% supination loss
💊 ER Pain Management
Oral NSAIDs + paracetamol sufficient for most. Sling in 90° flexion. Ice. Monitor for antecubital haematoma — can enlarge and compress surrounding structures. No reduction manoeuvre indicated.
🖐 Hook Test — sensitivity 81%, specificity ~100%
Patient flexes elbow to 90° and supinates. Hook your index finger behind the lateral edge of the biceps tendon from the lateral side. Normally you can hook ~1 cm of tendon cord. Nothing to hook = complete rupture.
Search YouTube 📹 Physiopedia reference
⚠ Don't miss
Partial tears are the diagnostic trap — persistent pain with full or near-full strength. The hook test may still feel a thinned cord. US is diagnostic. Don't miss the delayed antecubital ecchymosis appearing 24–48 h later.
Muscle & surgical emergencies
09
Iliopsoas / hip flexor tear
Misdiagnosed as a groin strain — femoral nerve compression turns it surgical
Muscle

High-speed kick or sprint. Deep anterior groin and lower abdominal pain. The serious variant: a large haematoma compressing the femoral nerve — presents with quadriceps weakness and anterior thigh numbness — this is a surgical emergency.

Evaluation
  • Deep groin/lower abdominal pain with resisted hip flexion
  • Palpable mass if haematoma present
  • Femoral nerve signs: quads weakness, anterior thigh numbness
  • MRI gold standard; US limited by depth
  • CT if anticoagulated or haematocrit dropping
Management
  • Most: conservative — rest, ice, NSAIDs 5–7 days
  • Crutches if weight bearing is painful
  • Femoral nerve signs: urgent CT/MRI + ortho/vascular
  • Lesser trochanter avulsion in young/active: surgical
💊 ER Pain Management
IV/oral NSAIDs + paracetamol. Position in hip flexion (pillow under knees) — this single intervention dramatically reduces pain by offloading the iliopsoas. Ice over lower abdomen/groin. Avoid active hip flexion for 2–3 weeks.
🖐 Resisted Hip Flexion Test
Patient supine, knee bent. Ask them to lift the thigh against your resistance. Deep groin pain = positive. Also: Thomas test position (passive hip extension with knee extended) stretches the iliopsoas and reproduces pain in complete tears.
Search YouTube 📹 Physiopedia reference
⚠ Don't miss
Retroperitoneal haematoma in anticoagulated patients (warfarin/DOAC) mimics this presentation. Dropping haematocrit + groin pain → urgent CT abdomen/pelvis. Femoral nerve palsy = surgical emergency — immediate referral.
10
Acute compartment syndrome
6-hour window · Never elevate · The emergency that permanently disables
Surgical Emergency

Most common after tibial shaft fractures, forearm fractures, and crush injuries. The fasciotomy window is 6 hours — after that, permanent muscle necrosis is inevitable. This is the orthopaedic emergency that kills limbs, not patients. GPs in rural areas must be prepared to recognise and initiate management.

Fasciotomy window6 hours from onset
Pressure threshold>30 mmHg or Δp <30 mmHg
The 6 Ps (in order of onset)
  • Pain out of proportion — earliest sign
  • Pressure — tense, woody compartment on palpation
  • Paresthesia — tingling in nerve distribution
  • Paralysis — late, very bad prognosis
  • Pallor — late sign
  • Pulselessness — very late, never wait for this
GP/ER Management
  • Remove ALL circumferential dressings and casts immediately
  • DO NOT elevate the limb (reduces perfusion pressure)
  • Keep limb at heart level
  • Measure compartment pressure if available
  • Call ortho NOW — IV access, prepare for theatre
  • Transfer immediately if surgery unavailable
💊 ER Pain Management
Do NOT withhold analgesia to "preserve the pain as a sign." The diagnosis is made clinically and by compartment pressure measurement — not by whether the patient is screaming. IV morphine titrated. Fasciotomy is the only definitive treatment.
🖐 Passive Stretch Test — the earliest clinical sign
Passively stretch muscles in the suspected compartment. Anterior leg: passively plantarflex the foot. Forearm: passively extend all fingers. Severe pain with passive stretch = compartment syndrome until proven otherwise. This appears before paresthesia and paralysis.
Search YouTube 📹 LITFL — Compartment Syndrome
⚠ Absolute rules — no exceptions
NEVER elevate. NEVER apply compression. NEVER cast or splint circumferentially. A tight cast after a tibial fracture that the patient says "feels too tight" is compartment syndrome until proven otherwise. Fasciotomy delayed >6 hours = permanent disability. Transfer immediately if you cannot operate.
